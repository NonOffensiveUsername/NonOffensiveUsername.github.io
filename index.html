Crullach

System reference document

After weeks at sea, land pierces the horizon. A mysterious continent awaits you, and with it the promise of wealth, power, and freedom unimaginable. Will you tame this frontier, or die like a dog? Only one thing is certain: Crullach has no sympathy.

Overview

Crullach (pronounced like "crew-lock" or "craw-latch") is a newly discovered island. As a player, you take control of an ambitious individual with designs on it. In typical Armada fashion, the mechanics are designed to faciliate competition in the military and economic dimensions while allowing for broad freedom in choosing one's objectives.

Mechanics

The two primary systems of Crullach are those governing characters and settlements. Characters are individual people while settlements are populated places. You interact with the game world through a player character and their associated characters and settlements; collectively, these form your faction.

Characters

Rather than tracking detailed statistics, characters in Crullach only require a level and, if their level is above zero, a class. Here's an example character sheet:

Millian Krain - Level 2 Mercenary

More rarely, a character could have multiple classes, each with their own level:

Godus Lanshaw - Lvl 3 Hunter / Lvl 1 Merchant

Finally, characters can have statuses. These are simple descriptors that explain how the character is different from others of their class. These can be inborn traits or temporary conditions. They can be chosen at the start of the game or assigned by the game master as needed. Statuses can also be removed by the game master. Examples of statuses are words like "wounded", "brave", "ugly" or "eagle-eyed".

Settlements

Controlling a character is only half of the game. In pursuing your you will doubtlessly wish to found and improve settlements. These will provide you with money, resources and manpower, as well as being the infrastructure you'll need to accomplish your goals. Settlements are represented almost exactly like characters; they have a class (or classes) and levels in these classes. But where a character might be a pikeman or a silversmith, a settlement is a village, or a trading post, or a fort. Also like characters, settlements can have statuses. Settlements are created through projects.

Creating a Faction

When designing your faction, the first step is to have a concept. Your character should have a reason to be exploring Crullach. They might be after money, or fame, or independence for a religious or ethnic group.

As a baseline, your player character is level one and has a class of your choice. Classes should be specific and represent concrete skills. Specificity and effectiveness are inversely correlated; a "fighter" might be comfortable with any weapon or armor but will be at a disadvantage in a duel against a "pikeman" using his weapon of choice, assuming the levels are the same.

In addition to your character and their class, you have 18 pips to spend on improving your situation. Optionally, you may gain 2 more pips by adding a negative status to your character. This status should be strictly harmful and not easily mitigated.

Pips can be spent on the following options. Each option can be taken as many times as you like and can afford.

  • 10 level 0 peasants. They believe in your cause and are ready to endure hardship for you.
  • A level 1 specialist. Choose their class. Their relationship with you is yours to decide; they could be a relative, business partner, friend, slave, etc.
  • 3 level 0 specialists. As above, but less skilled.
  • Increase the level of your player character or one of your specialists. The number of pips required is equivalent to their new level. For example, advancing a level one character to level 2 costs 2 pips. This is cumulative; taking a level 1 character to level 3 costs 2 + 3 = 5 pips.
  • Add a useful status to one of your characters.
  • 100 gold groens. See economics below for what this means and what you can spend them on.

Economics

The common currency of the empire is the silver drang, a small coin, a bit smaller and thicker than a dime, sufficient for a day's expenses in any city. 20 drangs is worth one golden groen, a month's wages for an unskilled laborer. More valuable coinage exists but is rarely seen, including the platinum vech (120 groens) which requires the expertise of a mage to mint.

Gameplay

Roleplay in Crullach is fluid and the passage of time will vary based on in game events. Resource management is done on a monthly basis. Each month you will allocate your available labor and resources towards various projects and handle events. Expect months to tick over every other day at 5pm.

Turns can vary greatly based on what is happening in the game world. However, you will almost always do the following:

  • Update your stockpiles of food and money
  • Divide your available labor among ongoing projects
  • Handle events

Make sure to properly organize your characters, settlements, and stockpiles for easy access and updating. Use your faction channel. It is extremely important to know what you have and where it is. Also be aware of your primary character's location, as news sometimes takes time to travel.

Projects

The core action of settlement management in Crisis on Crullach is assigning projects. A project is a defined task which requires a certain amount of labor and resoruces to complete. This definition is purposefully open ended. Here are some examples of projects:

  • Building a bridge
  • Mapping a hex
  • Planting or harvesting the year's crop
  • Choosing the best site for a new mine

Be creative! Your very survival may depend on it. As game master, my goal is to promote and reward clever solutions; use your resources well, and your reliance on lucky rolls should be minimal.

The biggest limitation to completing projects is labor. Labor is measured in manhours, a slightly abstracted representation of work done. A healthy human adult working at a sustainable pace can provide you with 250 manhours each month. In a pinch, you can push your people further, but beware the inevitable harm to morale and bodily health alike. Track your available monthly manhours, as this is your biggest resource. Each month, you will be informed about any changes in your available labor. Gaining or losing workers will affect the labor pool, but other factors can modify it as well. Harsh weather, disease, or malnutrition might reduce the efficiency of your workers, for example.

To be productive, laborers need the right tools, materials, and guidance. Tools may be possible to improvise, but this will lower efficiency. Gathering or moving materials may also add time to a project. Complicated projects will need to be overseen by a specialist.

Buildings

You can acquire buildings by completing projects. Buildings are like class levels for your settlements, allowing them to help you achieve your goals. Generally, a project to construct a building takes about 5,000 manhours per intended level of the building, plus one. Once you have one or more buildings on a hex, that hex becomes one of your settlements. Keep track of your settlements and the buildings they contain.

Like character classes, building types are not restricted to a premade list, but have a name to describe their purpose. Here are some possible buildings:

  • Farm Estate
  • Watch Tower
  • Trading Post
  • Fortification
  • Mine
  • Forge

Buildings may need upkeep over time to combat decay and accidents. Expect to spend more maintaining costlier buildings.

If you make sketches of your building and settlement layouts, they'll be slightly cheaper to construct. Having visuals will make exploration more interesting and immersive for everyone!

Specialists

Knowledge can be more valuable than any tool. Only those with the correct skills can exploit the wealth of Crullach. A specialist is a character with a class focused on some non-combat field like construction, farming, or prospecting. They can be purchased with pips as part of faction building or hired later. Because of the great variety of possible specialists, there are no rules for individual types. Instead, they are all governed by general rules with the details determined on a case by case basis.

The main functions of specialists are to advise you and oversee projects. As advisors, they are a valuable source of information. The mechanics behind important aspects of settlement building such as agriculture are purposefully opaque; consider specialists and their advice to be your way of peaking behind the veil and getting hints on the best course of action. For example, if you wish to farm, you will want an agricultural specialist to advise you on what crops would grow best on your land.

Specialists can always advise you, but to actually perform their trade they require tools. The quality of tools available to the specialist limit how well they can do their job. In gameplay terms, the quality of the specialist's tools caps their effective character level. A level 4 agriculturalist with tools of quality 2 will make rolls as if he is only second level. Depending on their task and specialty, they may require upkeep as well. The cost of upkeep, when applicable, is one fifth the cost of tools, per month. This is in addition to the costs of any raw materials the specialist is processing.

Agents

Your primary character can only be in one place at a time. To solve this problem, you can assign your underlings as agents and send them off to perform various tasks. Unlike projects, you won't know the status of the agent or his task until he returns to you, or not at all if the worst happens. You must properly supply your agent and give them carefully worded instructions, because once they've left, you cannot change them.

Some tasks are especiallly suitable for agents:

  • Trade. Unless your character is a merchant and personally goes about handling the faction's finances, an agent will be needed to import or export. A skilled negotiator could be invaluable for getting good deals for your faction.
  • Recruitment. An agent can be sent back to Exeter in order to seek out candidates and bring them back to Crullach. You must describe the type of people you want, and your agent will do their best to select and attract recruits. The amount of time taken and the likelihood of success depends on the rarity of fitting candidates (peasants are plentiful, master glassblowers are not), the relevant level of the agent, and how good a deal you're offering. "Be my servant" may attract the desparate or guillible, but the most useful people want their value to be recognized and rewarded.
  • Exploration. Being gone for extended periods in the wild is a good way to come back to a devastated settlement.
  • Diplomacy. There are no telephones on Crullach; aside from official visits, communication between distant settlements must happen through representatives. Make sure to pick someone you trust, and that they know what they're allowed to promise.

Advancement

Over time, characters can become more skilled. This will happen semi- randomly. Major accomplishments are especially likely to result in character advancement. Characters growing in ability may not be immediately beneficial. Specialists will usually need better tools to make use of a higher level. A disloyal character gaining levels may inspire them to plot against you!

Primary characters will level faster than others. When your primary character levels up, you may choose to advance one of their current classes or give them a new class at level 1.

This system is purposefully limited. The real way to become more powerful in Crullach is through building up your settlements, gaining wealth and growing your population. There are always opportunities!

Action Resolution

Occasionally, to facilitate drama, an action will require a roll of the dice. In such situations, the GM will state a difficulty and ask for a roll. If the character performing the action has a class relevant to it, a d6 is rolled and the level added to the result. Otherwise, a d4 is rolled. If the action is higher than the difficulty, the action has the intended result. If the result is equal to the difficulty, the GM may or may not impose a complication. Otherwise, the action does not have the intended result.

At the GM's discretion, characters can use their class as a similar class of a lower level. For example, a level 3 mercenary attempting to train soldiers could perform the task as a level 1 commander.

Some actions are directly opposed by another person. In such cases, both characters roll in the usual way. But in addition to beating the difficulty, their result must be higher than their opponents. Both sides of a contest failing to equal or exceed the difficulty results in a fumble and a continuation of the contest after a short time, possibly allowing others nearby to intervene.

When a roll is made in a dramatic situation, the GM will not reveal the difficulty number. After a roll but before the GM reveals the outcome, the player has the option of pushing it, giving them the opportunity to reroll. However, if the reroll is a failure, the situation will escalate; the consequences for failure will be more dire and the failing character will gain a negative status. The GM is the final arbiter of what counts as a dramatic situation.

Warfare

An army is an expensive undertaking. In a pinch, any able-bodied men can be mustered to fight, but they’ll perform relatively poorly and would be hopeless in an extended offensive operation. A competent fighting force that can project power must be actively maintained.

Raising Forces

Creating an army requires manpower, equipment, leadership, and time. Competent soldiers are classed characters; peons can only form militias. Turning ten peons into level 0 fighting men requires one month and a difficulty 4 roll from a trainer. Equipment must be purchased or made.

Soldiers can perform other tasks but must spend at least half of their time on active duty or exercises to remain ready. Failing this requirement disbands the force; reassembling it requires retraining and paying to repair unmaintained equipment proportional to the period of neglect.

Force Representation

Each soldier has combat pips based on their type, representing their basic combat capabilities. Pips come in 4 types:

  • Shock, representing the basic effectiveness of the soldier in close combat
  • Fire, for lethality at distances beyond a thrown weapon's reach
  • Thrust, for long piercing weapons designed to fight cavalry
  • Maneuver, for rapid response and flanking ability

An army's total shock, fire, and thrust pips added together is its strength.

Soldiers have a type representing how they are equipped and fight. The The pips are as described above; equipment cost is the type of soldier's kit and/or mount needed.

Exceptional Soldiers

Soldiers with a level of 1 or more are superior among men and project superior force in battle. The soldier's pips are multiplied by their level minus 1, except for maneuver which is unaffected. Take care not to rely overmuch on such men, lest the bulk of your power be brought low by a single stray arrow. A man who achieves too much glory might also question your leadership. If you are nothing without him, why should he take orders from you?

Exceptional equipment can further amplify the power of a mighty warrior. Equipment fine enough to multiply a soldier's effectiveness is not for sale at game start; the holders of such great power tend to guard it jealously. Such items are generaly unique and do not share mechanics.

Mass Combat

Except where specifically noted, rolls related to mass combat scenarios cannot be pushed.

Movement

Armies move depending on their type and stance. Foot soldiers move 10 miles per day. Add another 10 miles for mounts, and another 10 if soldiers travel recklessly, strung out and without scouting. Speed is halved for bad weather and halved again for bad terrain. Armies moving through unknown lands are liable to become lost.

Starting a Battle

When two opposing armies enter the same hex, the commanders roll a contest. Both parties roll in secret and may push their roll if desired. The following modifiers effect the contest:

  • Higher recon strength than opponent: +1
  • Noncombat stance: -2
  • On familiar terrain (possessing maps and experience): +1

In the case of a tie, both sides enter battle on equal footing. Otherwise, the winner has initiative equivalent to the margin by which he beats the loser. At this point a series of rounds are played until one or both forces retreat, surrender, or are destroyed.

Strategies and Modifiers

At the beginning of each round, the commanders of the two forces privately choose a strategy from the following list:

  • Attack. The default, with no special effects.
  • Defend. Gain +1 (or +2 with fire superiority) but gain no initiative if the round is won.
  • Charge. Gain +1 but take double casualties. Allows pushing rolls. If a pushed charge fails, take quadruple casualties!
  • Feint. Suffer -1 but gain double initiative on a victory.
  • Retreat. Disengage on a victory.
  • Route. Disengage, guaranteed, but take double casualties.
  • Parley. If the enemy commander accepts, there is a ceasefire and the two forces can negotiate. If the parley is rejected, the commander must change his strategy to defend, retreat, or route.

After choosing strategies, the commanders roll a contest with several modifiers.

Strength Advantage

The more powerful force, as by strength value (shock + fire + thrust), gains a bonus commensurate with its degree of advantage, calculated as the ratio of the larger force's strength to the smaller.

Strength Ratio Bonus
1 +0
1.5 +1
2 +2
3 +3

If the ratio falls in between two values, round down; 2.3 rounds to 2, 1.6 rounds to 1.5, etc. The progression continues indefinitely. The commander of the larger force gets the bonus; the commander of the smaller force does not receive a corresponding penalty.

Initiative

In a battle, one side can have initiative, a number representing a variety of factors which give a situational advantage. Only one side can have initiative, or neither. The side with initiative adds the amount to their roll.

Superiority

A side with more of certain class of units gains superiority in that type. If a commander's army has 50% more fire pips than his opponent, he has fire superiority and gains +1. If he has double the fire pips or more, he gains +2. Maneuver superiority works similarly, with a catch. Before the ratio of maneuver pips is calculated, each side subtracts the other's thrust pips from their own maneuver pips.

Damage

As armies take casualties, they lose fighting effectiveness. Every full 10% of a force's effectiveness lost adds a -1 penalty to the rolls of its commander.

Playing the Round

With all modifiers tallied, the commanders roll a contest. This roll can be pushed if the charge strategy was chosen. Calculate the margin by which the winner of the contest beat the loser (0 if there was no winner). Both sides take casualties based on this margin, according to the following table:

Margin Winner Casualties Loser Casualties
0 10% 10%
1 10% 15%
2 10% 20%
3 5% 20%
4 5% 25%
5 0% 25%
6-7 0% 30%
8-9 0% 35%
10+ 0% 40%

These casualties represent soldiers that are killed, injured, fleeing, etc. In addition to both sides taking casualties, the winner gains initiative. If they already have initiative, increase it by one. If the enemy has initiative, decrease it by one. If neither side has it, the winner gains it.

Special Cases

Some combinations of strategies have specific effects:

  • When both commanders choose to defend, the two forces merely skirmish. Halve casualties, rounding down to the nearest multiple of 5%.
  • If one side defends while the other retreats, the retreat automatically succeeds, and the retreating force takes half casualties.
  • If one side defends while the other routes, the routing force does not take double casualties.
  • If both sides choose retreat or route, they automatically succeed in disengaging and take no casualties.
Aftermath

If one side of the battle retreated or was destroyed, the other side takes the field. If both sides retreat, neither has the field. An army which has taken the field can regroup and recover their wounded, restoring half of the losses taken during the battle. If the losing army surrendered, the winner may allow them to do the same.

Either army, regardless of whether they have the field, can have a dedicated medical character make a roll; they regain the result of the roll as a percentage of their strength, up to the amount of casualties taken.

Non-recovered casualties are distributed among the forces by the GM, depending on the course of the battle. The army's pips are recalculated.

Lore

Crullach takes place in a medieval low fantasy world. Your character is from Exeter, westernmost continent of the known world. Exeter is de jure under the rule of the Quadrattion, a government lead by a council of four god-kings known as Tetrarchs. In reality, Exeter is divided into functionally independent states bound together by complex webs of tribute and obligation, with the tetrarchs acting as an influential alliance.

Extent of Imperial Control

Light red: Imperial crown territories. White: Nominal vassal states paying lip service to the central authorities. Grey: de jure imperial territories, minimal imperial presence. Black: barbarian lands, not claimed. Borders represent the formal boundaries of the various imperial provinces.

The Empire

Exeter. The Quadrattion. The Empire. Many names, but one truth. For the people of Exeter, the empire is completely synonymous with religion and the state at the conceptual level. True separatism is incredibly rare; most find the idea incomprehensible.

The story of the empire's founding is the source of its spiritual and political authority. Before the arrival of the tetrarchs, the story goes, Exeter was a land of ignorance. The people lived in fear, terrorized by foul gods and their dark priests. Darkness reigned for an unknowable length of time, for mankind was so primitive that it lacked a calendar. But one day, the tetrachs descended from heaven on angelic horses. They rallied the people, forming a great army against the gods, and overthrew the priests. The tetrarchs declared the beginning of year zero, marking the beginning of the new imperial order.

The government instituted by the tetarchs is an authoritarian theocracy, with themselves as the gods of its religion. According to church dogma, the current tetrarchs are one and the same with the founders of the empire. When a tetrarch's body begins to age, they go into seclusion for a time to be reborn in a new shape before emerging once more into public life. Insinuations that the tetrarch has perished and a new man taken his place is heresy. Heresy is treason. Treason is punishable by death.

Each tetrarch governs a sacred city from which they project power across Exeter. The cities are arranged in a perfect square, forming a sacred sigil of protection across the entire continent. From north to south, the tetrarchs are Snovius, Karl the Kind, Djinasterin, and Loem. Each sacred city shares the name of its ruling tetrarch. The word of any tetrarch is law anywhere on Exeter, but they usually refrain from acting directly in matters far from their sacred city. Despite their physical distance from each other, the divine nature of the tetrarchs allows them to coordinate flawlessly in managing the empire.

Cultures of Exeter

Pink: Exeter. Purple: Eskailish. Blue: Keruvian. Green: Norric. Brown: Burgognian. Grey: Cuskotoi.
